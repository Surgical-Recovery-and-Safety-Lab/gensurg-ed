Gastric Cancer

DEFINITION
Primary malignancy of the stomach, often a highly invasive disease associated with high mortality.

top D I A B M I M home 
<p> </p>
<hr> <b><a name="incidence">INCIDENCE<br>
    <br>
  </a></b> Common worldwide (4th biggest cancer, 2nd biggest killer)<br>
Reducing in West; used to be highest killer; now 1/3 what it was 35
yrs ago (poss. related to H. Pylori).<br>
(Trend is decreasing distal, increasing cardia/GOJ, increase in
diffuse type, represents 30% of neoplasms.<br>
<u><br>
  Geography</u><br>
Relatively low in West, high in SE Asia, Japan (decreasing with
Westernization), Russia, S America.<br>
2-4x in Polynesians.<br>
<u><br>
  Sex</u><br>
M2:F1.<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Age</span><br
  style="text-decoration: underline;">
Mean 60s at discovery<br>
Rare under 40 except in familial forms.<br>

<hr> <b><a name="aetiology">AETIOLOGY</a></b><br>
<br>
Gastric CA = 90% adenocarcinomas.<br>
10% others (mainly lymphoma, GIST).<br>
Occasional others: SCC, oat cell carcinoma, carcinoid, mesodermal
tumours etc.<br>
<br>
<u>AdenoCa Predisposing factors</u><br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Genetic</span><br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Familial Gastric cancer</span><br>
1. Hereditary diffuse gastric cancer<br>
- 2 or more cases in first/second degree relatives; one diagnosed
before 50<br>
- 3 or more independent of age<br>
- E-cadherin gene inactivating mutation<br>
- esp common in Maori.<br>
2. Familial diffuse gastric cancer<br>
3. Familial intestinal gastric cancer<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Inflammatory</span><br>
Chronic gastritis usual background.<br>
<i style="font-weight: bold;">H. pylori</i><span style="font-weight:
  bold;"> main cause of this (3-4x risk if infected).</span><br>
- risk proportionate to H pylori serum levels.<br>
Atrophy of gastric mucosa (decreased acidity, more bacteria, produce
toxins), assoc. with pernicious anaemia.<br>
Also chronic peptic ulcers.<br>
- not significantly (h. pylori is), but 1% associated by virtue of
chronic inflammatory change.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Nutritional</span><br>
Vitamin C deficiency.<br>
Low fruit and veg, high starch.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Toxins</span><br>
Smoking.<br>
Dietary toxins (nitrosamines, ?polycyclic hydrocarbons, dried,
smoked and salted food).<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Iatrogenic</span><br>
Previous gastric surgery.<br>
- 2% risk of cancer at a gastroduodenal anastomosis<br>
- typically 15-20y after surgery; young surgical at risk.<br>
- chronic gastritis and atrophy, fundic gland polyps, hyperplastic
polyps also more common in the remnant.<br>
- both intestinal and diffuse types, 40% early, <br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Other</span><br>
Also in blood group A people; ?why.<br>
Menetrier's Disease<br>
- rare; rugal hypertrophy and hyperplasia, hypochlorhydria and
protein-losing enteropathy.<br>
- cancer in 10%, often diagnosed within 12mo of each other.<br>

<hr> <b><a name="biologicalbehaviour">BIOLOGICAL BEHAVIOUR</a></b><br>
<u><br>
  Pathology</u><br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">AdenoCa</span><br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;"><br>
  Lauren Classification</span>:<br>
- <span style="text-decoration: underline;">Intestinal</span> (well
differentiated; mucosal origin) <br>
- or <span style="text-decoration: underline;">diffuse</span>
(poorly differentiated; lamina propria origin)<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;"><br>
  1. </span><span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">Intestinal</span><span
  style="font-weight: bold;"> </span>(20% 5-yr survival)<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">-</span> associated with chronic
gastritis and intestinal metaplasia, H. pylori.<br>
- tend to be well differentiated and expansile<br>
- high risk populations, M&gt;F 2:1; older pts; declining<br>
- tends to form glands and met via blood.<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;"><br>
  2.</span><span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">
  Diffuse</span> are ominous (10% 5-yr survival)<br>
- no elements secrete mucous; unclear cause; ?genetic, ?h. pylori<br>
- tend to be poorly differentiated and infiltrative<br>
- tend to affect F&gt;M, younger pts and may be more genetically
determined.<br>
- spreads via submucosal infiltration<br>
<br>
<u>Pathogenesis</u><br>
<span style="font-style: italic;"> Classical development theorized
  as: </span><br>
-&gt; H pylori infection<br>
-&gt; Acute gastritis<br>
-&gt; chronic active gastritis<br>
-&gt; atrophic gastritis<br>
-&gt; intestinal metaplasia<br>
-&gt; dysplasia<br>
-&gt; gastric cancer<br>
This is not fixed; 60-70% of mild-mod dysplasia may revert to
normal; severe = much more likely to progress.<br>
This only applies to intestinal type; diffuse tend not to have
association with chronic gastritis / atrophy.<br>
<br>
<u> Natural History</u><br>
Arises in the mucosa.<span style="font-style: italic;"><br>
  <br>
  <span style="font-weight: bold;">Early Gastric Ca</span><br>
</span>When confined to the mucosa or submucosa, regardless of lymph
involvement.<br>
5 yr survival of such cancer good (90%), diagnosis in West very poor
(10% early)<br>
- in Japan, screening with double contrast barium meal and endoscopy
means more (e.g. 30%) detected early.<br>
- better when confined to submucosa (90%) vs submucosa (75%) - 15 yr
survivals.<br>
Predominantly distal stomach, most 2-5cm; if &lt;5mm then 'minute'.<span
  style="font-weight: bold;"><br>
</span>LN risk &lt;5% with mucosal disease; 20% with submucosal
invasion.<br>
- higher risk with ulceration, larger tumours<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;"><br>
  Early Gastric Ca Macroscopic Classification:</span><br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Type I:</span> protuberant<br>
- sessile, smooth nodules with a broad stalk, often pale<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Type II</span>:
superficial (a=elevated; b=flat; c=depressed)<br>
- slightly <br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Type III</span>:
ulcerating<br>
Exophytic tumours = better prognosis (more intestinal type);
Ulcerating = diffuse.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">Location</span><br>
Commonest in the antrum (40%), but diffuse cancers of prox stomach
(30%) becoming more common in West, unclear why.<br>
Spread into the duodenum is a poor prognostic sign (5-yr survival
approaching 5%).<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">Spread</span><br>
Intramural, direct extraluminal growth, lymph, and mets.<br>
- involves regional nodes at least in 3/4 at presentation.<br>
Intragastric spread often proximal &gt; distal<br>
- pylorus is a partial barrier; tumor in 25% past bulb still.<br>
Later liver, bone, brain, lung.<br>
5 yr survival rate for advanced Ca is below 15%.<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;"><span style="font-weight:
    bold;">Subtypes : Borrman Classification</span><br>
</span>Four subdivisions correlate loosely with natural history and
outcome<br>
In order of nastiness:<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">I. Polypoid carcinomas</span><br
  style="font-style: italic;">
- large, bulky intraluminal growths that tend to metastasize late.<br>
- often soft and red in colour with a broad base<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">II. Ulcerating / excavated
  carcinoma</span><br style="font-style: italic;">
- deep penetrating ulcer-tumor; all layers deep<br>
- flat edges instead of rolled edges<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">III. Ulcerative with elevated
  margins, infiltrated</span><br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">IV. Linitis plastica</span> type<br>
- all layers thick, spreads with a marked desmoplastic reaction.<br>
- cure is rare, spread advanced<span style="font-style: italic;"></span><br>
<u><br>
</u>*There is also a lymphoepithelioma-like carcinoma, often
associated with EBV, with a relatively good prognosis.<u><br>
  <br>
  <span style="font-weight: bold;">Genetics</span></u><br>
Disruption of the genome, participation of oncogenes, mutation of
tumor-suppressor genes.<u><span style="font-weight: bold;"></span><br>
</u>p53 is most common abnormality; tumour suppressor gene involved
in preserving stability during DNA turnover<u><br>
</u>- if not, then usually microsatellite instability; different
pathway for collecting genetic abnormalities.<br>
APC and DCC genes also commonly affected.<br>
E-cadherin (cellular adhesion molecules) reduced expression in 90%,
esp diffuse types.<br>
Familial clustering in HNPCC and familial gastric cancer.<br>
<u><br>
  <span style="font-weight: bold;">Complications</span></u><br>
Late cancers risk perforation.<br>
Local perf with abscess, internal or external fistula possible,
especially in the ulcerating form.<br>

<hr> <b><a name="manifestations">MANIFESTATIONS</a></b><br>
<br>
<b>SYMPTOMS</b><br>
<br>
<u>Local</u><br>
No symptoms when superficial and curable.<br>
Later varies from:<br>
- vague non-specific symptoms e.g. early satiety, heaviness, not
identified as pain.<br>
- to severe steady abdo pain (though this often slight and can be
relieved by food).<br>
May have had dyspepsia for years, but persistence is new.<br>
<br>
Anorexia (often w meat), dyspepsia, <br>
Nausea, vomiting (severe if near pylorus), and altered bowel habit
possible.<br>
<br>
Less frequently dysphagia (if involves cardia)<br>
Haemorrhage (haematemesis / malaena), perforation<br>
<br>
<u>General</u><br>
Loss of weight and strength, malaise.<br>
Frequently anaemic symptoms.<br>
<br>
<u>Metastatic</u><br>
Local spread.<br>
Symptoms from lymph, liver, bones, brain, lung.<br>
<br>
<b>SIGNS</b><br>
<br>
<u>Observe</u><br>
May be jaundice, cachexia, anaemia.<br>
If metastatis - appropriate signs to liver, bone, brain, lung.<br>
Associated with dermatomyositis (non-suppurative inflammation of
skin) and acanthosis nigricans (thickened, hyperpigmented skin at
flexures).<br>
<br>
<u>Palpate</u><span style="font-style: italic;">; Advanced Disease</span><br>
Palpable tumour usually means diagnosis too late.<br>
Virchow's Node (left supraclavicular fossa); Sister Mary Joseph
Nodule <br>
?Large liver <br>
?Malignant ascites.<br>
Rectal or PV may show Blumer's shelf (pre-rectal mets) in advanced
disease.<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Metastatic</span><br>
Consider also lungs, bone, brain.<br>

<hr> <b><a name="investigations">INVESTIGATIONS</a></b><br>
<u><br>
  Endoscopy</u><br>
Gastroscopic biopsy is standard, with biopsy for all pts with ulcer
(6+ bxs)<br>
- GC ulcers are suspect.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;"><br>
  Role of EUS</span><br>
Can show depth of tumor (80% accuracy), and lymph nodes (only 50%
accuracy!)<br>
But specifically useful for the showing depth of invasion<br>
- differentiating T2 tumours, which need neoadjuvant chemo and may
be amenable to EMR in specialist centres.<br>
<br>
<u>Imaging</u><br>
Staging depends on CT, EUS and laparoscopy<br>
- CT shows metastatic disease<br>
AXR - stomach rigid if 'leather bottle'.<br>
Ba meal study (double contrast)<br>
<u><br>
</u><u>Staging Laparoscopy</u><br>
Highly advisable; done as a day stay procedure.<br>
Shows peritoneal metastatic disease in 30% of pts with normal
cross-sectional imaging.<br>
Collect peritoneal washings<br>
- if +ve, predicts early peritoneal recurrence.<br>
- in absence of visible mets, decreases survival from 98.5 months to
14.8 months,<br>
<u><br>
</u><u><i>Role of PET</i><u><br>
  </u></u>Less utility in gastric cancer as many are not FDG avid.<br>
- only 60%; vs ~90% of esophageal cancers.<br>
More likely in intestinal-type tumours vs diffuse signet-ring type
cancers.<u><br>
  <br>
  Haematology</u><br>
Leucocytosis possible.<br>
May be picked up on FOB (50% +ve)<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Markers</span><br
  style="text-decoration: underline;">
CEA +ve in 65%, usually with extensive disease<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Histology</span><br
  style="text-decoration: underline;">
Dysplasia in general is divided into low-grade and high-grade<br>
- on basis of cytological vs architectural<br style="font-weight:
  bold;">
Spread correlates with degree of differentiation.<br>
- glandular = better than 'diffuse'<br>
- 'diffuse' type often associated with substrantial stromal
component.<br>
- 'Signet ring' = poorly differentiated; bad; more in women, younger
patients<br>
Lymphovascular invasion is also prognostic.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Staging</span><span
  style="font-style: italic;"></span><br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">TNM</span><br
  style="text-decoration: underline;">
<span style="font-style: italic;">Tumour</span><br>
Tx = unassessable.<br>
T0 = no primary tumour evident.<br>
Tis = in-situ (intra-epithelial; not invading lamina propria).<br>
T1 = Invades lamina propria or submucosa.<br>
T2 = Invades muscularis (T2a) or subserosa (T2b)<br>
T3 = Invades serosa / visceral peritoneum, but not adjacent
structures<br>
T4 = Invades adjacent structures.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Nodes<br>
</span>Nx = unassessable<br>
N0 = no regional nodes<br>
N1 = 1-6 regional nodes<br>
N2 = 7-15 nodes<br>
N3 = &gt;15 nodes<br>
[need 15 nodes in specimen for accurate staging]<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Mets</span><br>
Mx = unassessable<br>
M0 = no mets<br>
M1 = distant mets<span style="font-style: italic;"><span
    style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-style: italic;"></span></span><br>
</span> <br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Staging</span><br>
0&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Tis<br>
IA&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; T1N0M0<br>
IB&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; T1N1M0, or T2a/bN0M0<br>
II&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; T1N2M0, T2N1M0, T3N0M0<br>
IIIA T2N2M0, T3N1M0, T4N0M0<br>
IIIB&nbsp; T3N2M0<br>
IV &nbsp;&nbsp; T4N1-3M0, T1-3N3M0, anyTanyNM1<br>
- ie T4 or N3 or M1 is very bad news.&nbsp; <br>

<hr> <b><a name="management">MANAGEMENT</a></b><br>
Multidisciplinary; gastroenterologists, surgeons, oncologists,
radiologists.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Screening? </span><br>
Done in Japan&nbsp; with mobile X-ray unit and double contrast
study, increasing early diagnosis and survival rates.<br>
However, not justified as overall benefit is minimal, and at present
even in dyspeptic sufferers, not sufficiently feasible.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Surgical<br>
  <br>
</span><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Principles</span><br>
Surgical excision remains the cornerstone of curative therapy.<br>
- possible in &lt;25%<br>
Want an R0 resection, radical lymphadenectomy, 5cm margin (due to
submucosal spread).<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">What do do with R1 resection?<br>
  <span style="font-style: italic;"></span></span>Controversial.&nbsp;



Decide based on patient's overall condition and treatment goals <br>
- makes little difference when there is advanced disease.<br>
- but if early tumour / good lymph status and fit for surgery,
consider it.<span style="font-style: italic;"><span
    style="font-style: italic;"></span><br>
</span><br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Procedure</span><span
  style="font-style: italic;"></span><br>
See <a href="Gastrectomy.html">gastrectomy</a><br>
<br>
<i>Role of proximal partial gastrectomy?</i><br>
Intractable reflux limits utility.<br>
Avoided generally.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Extent of Lymphadenectomy?</span><br
  style="font-style: italic;">
Remains controversial.<br>
D0 = no nodes, i.e. palliative resection<br>
D1 = excision of perigastric nodes<br>
D2 = nodes along trunks of coeliac axis.<br>
Radical in Japan, where evidence supports D2 clearance.<br>
Most Western surgeons are skeptical of radical lymph resections.<br>
- Dutch Gastric Cancer Group failed to show benefit of D2&gt;D1 in
survival or recurrence-free survival, but did show higher morbidity.<br>
- Similar results from British Medical Research Council Trial.<br>
--&gt; distal pancreatectomy and splenectomy were included;
contributed to morbidity.<br>
--&gt; when not included, non-randomized trials show comparable
morbidity D1=D2 and improved survival.<br>
Aggressive lymphadenectomy allows adequate staging.<br>
- No. +ve nodes, regardless of location determines survival.<br>
Need at least 15 nodes for accurate staging and improved survival at
all disease stages.<br>
--&gt; likely due to less understaging<br>
However, new long-term follow-up data emerging that may state
very-long-term survival benefit weighs in favour of D2, unclear.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Frozen Section?</span><br>
Good practice to send routine frozen section to ensure R0 resection.<br>
- before constructing anastomosis; may need extension.<br>
<br>
<i>EMR?</i><br>
Emerging role, currently mainly in Japan where tumours diagnosed
very early.<br>
Little role in West where most are found late<br>
Also require highly specialist center and some may not be amenable,
such as GOJ tumours or at high curvatures.<br>
<br>
<span style="text-decoration: underline;">Palliative Surgery?</span><br
  style="text-decoration: underline;">
Controversial.<br>
No role at present; no evidence of survival and cannot be justified
for QOL.<br>
Significant morbidity (50% complications)<br>
Observational studies show only 10% of those who have operations
aborted will undergo subsequent surgical intervention.<br>
If obstructs, stent is best option.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Adjuvant Therapy</span><span
  style="font-weight: bold; text-decoration: underline;"><br
    style="font-weight: bold; text-decoration: underline;">
</span>Many R0 patients have recurrent disease in &lt;2 yrs, hence
adjuvant focus.<br>
Survival benefit was initially not shown on RCTs for chemo/rad.<br>
Intergroup 116 trial showed both overall and relapse-free survival
better with 5-FU and lecovorin with external beam radRx.<br>
--&gt; now standard of care in US<br>
--&gt; however, muddied by poor quality and standardization of
medical therapy; 54% had R0, only 10% R2.<br>
Newer trials from Japan beginning to show promise using different
agents.<br>
- however, relevance to non-D2 population in West unclear.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Bottom line:</span><br>
When accurately staged and standardized lymph resection, adjuvant
chemorad not known to clearly surpass that of surgery alone.<br>
This is the focus of the Top Gear study, contributing out of
Australia<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Neoadjuvant Therapy</span><br>
&gt;1/3 patients cannot tolerate post-op chemo; better tolerated
pre-op.<br>
Better tolerance, can assess disease response in-vivo, downstaging
potential.<br>
MAGIC trial (British Medical Council Adjuvant Gastric Infusional
Chemo) = periop epirubicin, cisplatin, 5-FU, vs surgery alone =
better overall and progression free survival, and better
resectability.<br>
<span style="font-style: italic;">Bottom line</span>:<br>
--&gt; Give neoadjuvant chemo for patients with T2 or higher
tumours.<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Prognosis</span><br>
Nomogram on p84 of Cameron.<br>
- Age (the young and very old do worse)<br>
- Sex (Men worse)<br>
- Primary site (GEJ bad)<br>
- Lauren (Diffuse worse)<br>
- Size (20 = bad)<br>
- Nodes (5 = bad, &gt;10 = very bad)<br>
- Depth (deeper = very bad)<br>
<br>
<span style="font-weight: bold;">Palliation</span><br>
Many elderly with lymph spread, contra-indicating surgery.<br>
Palliation, pain relief, relief of dysphagia, referral to med onc.<br>
In advanced metastatic disease, mild chemo may help; 5-fluorouacil
and cisplatin.<br>
Generally gastric CA radioresistive, though it has some role in
palliation.<br>
